About Me

I am a third year student at Imperial College London currently pursuing an MEng in Electrical and Electronics Engineering. My passion for electronics has instigated the pursuit of a plethora of projects, both within and outside of my academic curriculum. These endeavours have provied me with invaluable experience in both low level and high level programming languages, a rooted understanding of analogue electronics as well as nurtured my skills in digital logic and embedded systems.

My hobbies include Martial Arts(1st dan black belt Shotokan Karate), Pro-Kabaddi(full contact indoor sport which originated in India) and video game development. The latter has motivated me to help a few of my classmates and friends co-found the Imperial College Union Game Development Society, of which I am currently Treasurer.

EE Bug

The design objective for this first year project was to build a line following robot, given a PCB, Breadboard, two motors and a Â£10 budget. The approach I determined to be most suitable for this assignment was to utilise both analogue and digital components. In particular, the motor supply voltages were controlled by an ATTiny85 8-pin microprocessor, whereas the output from each LDR sensor was processed in the analogue domain before being passed to the microprocessor input. This task inculcated an appreciation for the myriad of challenges faced when dealing with non-ideal analogue components, electrical crosstalk introduced by connecting wires, as well as back currents generated by motors. Overcoming them demanded a rigorous understanding of the various implementations of diodes, capacitors and active components such as MOSFETs to design analogue filters and protection circuits.

Elaborating on the digital aspect of this design, it must first be stated that this device was programmed in Embedded C, using a Pocket AVR Programmer. This was intentionally chosen over Arduino, to gain experience in low level programming concepts, such as bitwise logic and shifts.

In addition to the culmination of my technical skills acquired over that year, this challenge provided me with early exposure to the real world challenges such as waiting times for components to arrive, limited testing equipment, and developing synergy with my teammates.

Real Time Speech Enhancement

This project was the final assignment in one of my third year modules, Real Time Digital Signal Processing. The ultimate deliverable was a real time noise cancellation digital filter, written in C. This was implemented on a TI DSK chip and tested against a range of audio files, each containing an identical recital of a specific dialogue in the presence of different noise sources. These sources took a wide range of intensities, from inside a car with the windows down to in front of a Phantom 4 fighter jet as it drove down a runway. This variety was to test the versatility of the noise rejection algorithm, both in decibel attenuation and in frequency range of operation.

I would be remiss to talk about this project without mentioning the high degree of compromise that came with it. More specifically, all methods employed involved a trade off between noise attenuation and the introduction of musical noise. Musical noise is high pitched noise artefacts introduced by spectral subtraction, the real time filtering method investigated in this coursework. Consequently, a multitude of different filtering algorithms were explored, derived from previous research in the field, as well as a few solutions innovated by myself and my lab partner. The technical details shall not be discussed here, however it should be stated that this project vastly improved my understanding of highly efficient DSP programming techniques, such as circular buffers circular buffers and overlap and add fourier analysis.

Machine Learning

In my third year of university I opted for a Machine Learning course, the practical elements of which were coded in MATLAB. Although several assignments were undertaken over the duration of this course, the one that proved most interesting to me was the development of a Collaborative Filtering Algorithm. This is similar to the Netflix Prize Competition of the late 2000's. The premise is utilising scored provided by highly correlated samples to predict the score of a new sample. In layman's terms, it is an algorithm which predicts a user's rating of a show based on the ratings for this show provided by other users who like similar shows. In contrast to more basic machine learning techniques, which only use a single user over multiple shows, this method is able to extract the same amount of predictive power with vastly fewer data points.

Delving a bit further into the technical details, I designed this collaborative filter using Stochastic Gradient Descent(SGD). SGD uses a mathematical manipulation of the pure Gradient Descent formula to iteratively use single points, in favour of using the entire dataset. The maths won't be elaborated on here, however the core motivation behind this technique is its operational efficiency, converging to a solution much faster than the traditional method, without sacrificing the precision of the result.

From a more general point of view, this assignment has endowed me with a high level of competency in MATLAB, introducing me to concepts such as logical indexing and various innovative algorithms and programming techniques tailored for Big Data problems.

Stochastic Modelling and Adaptive Filtering

Another optional MATLAB based course done in my third year, Advanced Signal Processing(ASP) has sparked my interest in practical implementation of stochastic modelling. Throughout the progression of the module, I was introduced to the fundamentals of Autoregressive(AR) and Moving Average(MA) modelling. Ultimately these concepts were stepping stones to arrive at the topic of Adaptive Filters.

There was no single task for this topic, however they all fall under the umbrella of audio signal processing, ranging from vowel speech recognition to vinyl denoising. The latter was particularly interesting, as it shared several underlying principles covered in the Machine Learning course discussed previously, but approached from a stochastic perspective. Moreover, it involved removing an extremely non-stationary noise element from the vinyl(an aperiodic beep), which demanded a compromise between learning/convergence rate and steady state error.

Ultimately, ASP has provided me with tremendous insight into MATLAB's signal processing and statistics toolboxes, as well as a deeper appreciation for the link between statistics and signal processing.

NeuroSpell

My second year group design project was to design and build a Brain Computer Interface(BCI) speller. Its target market is chronically motor impaired individuals who wish to use a keyboard to communicate. The project was divided into two major sections, hardware and software. My primary role was to design the hardware for this device, i.e the circuits for a functional Electroencephalogram(EEG).

This challenge required a great deal of research into practical analogue circuit design, going above and beyond the knowledge obtained in my university curriculum. This was largely due to the fact that this device ought to be robust to a wide range of operating conditions. A case in point is the extremely low Signal to Noise Ratio(SNR) of brain signals read through electrodes on the scalp, coupled with the discrete locations of the nodes. Ultimately, a suite of analogue filters were implemented, ranging from very simple first order filters, to higher order butterworth filters.

Ultimately what I gained from this experience was not limited to improved analogue knowledge, but also soft skills such as teambuilding, fostering morale amongst the team members when progress seemed to be negligible and deadlines fast approaching, and the ability to work on a project much larger than any of our individual roles. The latter was especially important as it introduced me to the challenges faced when logistics don't coincide, such as when one team finishes their part much earlier than the other, as well as how to develop contingencies for these situations.
